Biomedical Research on Reverse Walking

Biomechanical and Neuromuscular Gait Analysis

Explore Project

Introduction

Human walking is a coordinated activity involving interaction between sensory input, motor control, and musculoskeletal function. Although forward walking is commonly studied in gait analysis, reverse walking provides additional insight into neuromuscular coordination because it requires greater balance control and proprioceptive awareness.

Unlike forward walking, reverse walking limits visual guidance and demands more precise muscle coordination to maintain stability. For this reason, backward walking is often used in rehabilitation training and gait assessment.

In everyday activities, walking is often combined with secondary cognitive tasks such as texting or reading on a mobile phone. These dual-task conditions divide attention and may affect gait stability.

Project Abstract

Walking is a complex motor activity that depends on proper coordination between the musculoskeletal and nervous systems. Reverse walking is more challenging than forward walking because it reduces visual feedback and increases the demand for balance and neuromuscular control.

The difficulty further increases when a secondary cognitive task, such as mobile phone usage, is introduced during reverse walking. This study investigates biomechanical and neuromuscular adaptations during forward walking, reverse walking, and reverse walking while using a mobile phone.

Motion capture, force plate analysis, and surface electromyography (EMG) were used to evaluate temporal-spatial parameters, joint kinematics, ground reaction forces, and muscle activity.

The observations showed reduced walking speed, shorter step length, increased stance duration, and increased gastrocnemius activation during reverse walking, with more pronounced effects during the dual-task condition. These findings indicate that reverse walking under cognitive load imposes higher balance and neuromuscular demands.

Why This Study Matters

The study demonstrates the usefulness of dual-task reverse walking in evaluating gait stability and identifying motor control impairments. It also provides valuable insight for rehabilitation, sports training and functional mobility assessment.
